TYPOLOGY: LAYERING VIA INTERNATIONAL WIRE TRANSFERS
Source: FATF Report on Money Laundering Through the Banking Sector | FinCEN Advisory FIN-2018-A002
Category: Layering Stage Money Laundering

DEFINITION:
Layering is the second stage of money laundering in which criminals attempt to distance illicit funds from their source through a series of complex financial transactions. International wire transfers are a preferred layering mechanism due to transaction speed, cross-jurisdictional complexity, and the challenges of coordinating AML responses across multiple regulatory regimes.

KEY RED FLAGS:
- Wire transfers to multiple high-risk jurisdictions in rapid succession
- Beneficiary countries on FATF grey or black lists
- Inconsistency between stated business purpose and actual wire patterns
- New accounts immediately initiating large cross-border wires (short account tenure)
- Multiple wires to the same foreign beneficiary with slight amount variations
- Use of correspondent banking to obscure the originator trail
- Beneficiary entities with no verifiable online presence or business registration
- Wires routed through jurisdictions with strong banking secrecy laws
- PEP or PEP-adjacent recipients in the wire chain

GEOGRAPHIC RISK FACTORS (HIGH-RISK JURISDICTIONS):
- UAE: Free zones (JAFZA, DAFZA) used for opaque layering structures
- Jordan: Proximity to conflict zones; informal value transfer systems prevalent
- Turkey: Significant hawala activity; transit point for Middle Eastern funds
- Cayman Islands: Offshore financial centre; beneficial ownership opacity
- British Virgin Islands, Panama, Seychelles: Shell company proliferation

PATTERN VARIANTS:
1. Boomerang Wires: Funds sent abroad and returned to different domestic account
2. Round-Tripping: Money leaves and re-enters US appearing as foreign investment
3. Nested Correspondent Abuse: Using smaller correspondent banks to obscure originator
4. Shell Company Chain: Multiple shell entities in different jurisdictions receive/forward funds

VELOCITY AND CONCENTRATION INDICATORS:
- More than 3 cross-border wire transfers to same country in 30 days without documented business purpose
- Wire transfers beginning within 30 days of account opening
- Total cross-border wire volume exceeds 200% of account's historical baseline
- Single beneficiary receiving >60% of total outgoing wire volume

DOCUMENTATION RED FLAGS:
- Customer cannot produce invoices when requested
- Invoice amounts inconsistent with market rates for stated goods/services
- Vague descriptions: "consulting," "management fees," "services rendered"
- Counterparty entity registered after the customer's account was opened

REGULATORY TRIGGERS:
SAR filing warranted when two or more layering indicators co-occur with geographic risk. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) required for all Tier 1 correspondent relationships with high-risk jurisdictions.

CASE TYPOLOGY EXAMPLE:
Account opened 6 months ago by "import/export consultant" shows 7 wire transfers in 72 hours to three high-risk countries (UAE, Jordan, Turkey), all structured below $10,000, totaling $68,150. Beneficiary entities include one (Al-Najah Trading LLC) where the account holder's spouse is listed as a director — creating a PEP-adjacent relationship. Pattern matches FATF Mutual Evaluation findings on UAE and Jordan.
