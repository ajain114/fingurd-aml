TYPOLOGY: STRUCTURING (SMURFING)
Source: FinCEN Advisory FIN-2020-A003 | FATF Guidance on AML/CFT Measures
Category: Placement Stage Money Laundering

DEFINITION:
Structuring is the deliberate act of breaking up financial transactions into smaller amounts, specifically to avoid Currency Transaction Report (CTR) filing requirements that apply to cash transactions of $10,000 or more. This is a federal crime under 31 U.S.C. § 5324 regardless of whether the funds are from legal or illegal sources.

KEY RED FLAGS:
- Multiple transactions just below $10,000 threshold (typically $8,000–$9,999)
- Same-day or consecutive-day transactions at different branches or ATMs
- Transactions split across multiple accounts or family members
- Customer requests information about CTR thresholds
- Round numbers or highly consistent amounts across multiple transactions
- No plausible business explanation for transaction fragmentation
- Rapid deposits followed by equally rapid withdrawals

PATTERN VARIANTS:
1. Classic Structuring: Single individual making multiple sub-$10K deposits
2. Smurfing: Multiple individuals ("smurfs") each depositing sub-$10K amounts
3. Reverse Structuring: Breaking up large withdrawals to avoid detection
4. Commingled Structuring: Mixing legitimate and illicit funds across multiple transactions

STATISTICAL INDICATORS:
- Standard deviation of transaction amounts is abnormally low (amounts are suspiciously consistent)
- Bimodal distribution of amounts with cluster just below $10,000
- Transaction frequency spikes with inverse relationship to individual transaction amounts
- Velocity increase without corresponding business event

REGULATORY GUIDANCE:
Per FinCEN, financial institutions must file a SAR when they know, suspect, or have reason to suspect a transaction involves structuring. The willful element is NOT required for SAR filing — reasonable suspicion is sufficient. Filing deadline: 30 days from detection, 60 days if no suspect identified.

PREDICATE CRIMES COMMONLY ASSOCIATED:
Drug trafficking, tax evasion, fraud, human trafficking, bribery and corruption

CASE TYPOLOGY EXAMPLE:
A business account shows 7 outgoing wire transfers over 72 hours, all to foreign counterparties, with individual amounts ranging from $9,500 to $9,900 — all below the $10,000 CTR threshold. The total of $68,150 would trigger CTR filing if conducted as a single transaction. No invoice or business purpose documentation provided by customer.
